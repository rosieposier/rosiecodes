About

Back in the good old days of going on the computer I spent many hours building everything from elaborate RollerCoaster Tycoon parks to custom MySpace themes.

I later ended up studying coding because I love learning about and making things with computers.

There is so much to explore, even solving the tiniest problem or building the simplest tool can unexpectedly lead me to find a different method of doing something or discover some new tech that I really like.

Come on computer, we're going on a grand adventure.

I wanted to build something with a retro aesthetic but make it as light and fast as possible. With React + Redux adding up to 140 kB, I decided against using any frameworks or third-party libraries.

I ended up with a 28.5 kB SPA (technically - it is only one page and it does dynamically update content) before I added the image files. I wanted to get the final compressed size as low as I could, so I created all the artwork and icons with that in mind.

I opted for inline CSS and a preloaded WOFF 2.0 font, lazy-loaded all images, and kept JS minimal and deferred. Once minified and gzipped, all files added to a grand total of 67-ish kB with a Lighthouse performance score of 99. Not a bad start!

I also wanted to meet WCAG 2.2 standards to at least AA, and most sections are actually to AAA conformance. If you find any accessibility or other issues please do let me know and I'll fix them ASAP.

When I'm not hunched over a computer I can often be found hanging out at my local powerlifting gym. I'm also an open source maintainer for openpowerlifting, a competition result archive.

I've been competing for over five years, and when I started learning to code I unexpectedly found a lot of similarities. "What's the best programming language to learn" can be found a hundred times over on every forum, just like "what's the best exercise to do" is asked at least once a day by new gym-goers.

To me the answer to both questions is just choose one and try really hard. There will always be something better - faster processors, stiffer knee sleeves - but doing something suboptimally teaches you more than endlessly researching the most optimal way to do it.

Don't let trying to be perfect stop you from just trying.
